Disease occurs from a breakdown in the balance between three factors (as per Snieszko, 1970):

  • Host: The fish's own health, immunity, and stress level.
  • Pathogen: The virulence and concentration of the disease-causing agent.
  • Environment: Water quality parameters like temperature, oxygen, and stocking density.

Stress is a primary trigger for disease outbreaks. Key points include:

  • Stressors include high stocking density, poor water quality, and handling.
  • The stress response is mediated by the Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Interrenal (HPI) axis.
  • This axis stimulates the production of corticosteroids (stress hormones).
  • While adaptive, these hormones are immuno-suppressive, lowering the fish's ability to fight off pathogens.

The shift to intensive systems has increased disease risk:

  • High Stocking Density: Increases stress and facilitates rapid pathogen transmission.
  • Water Quality Issues: High waste production can lead to low oxygen and high ammonia, stressing fish.
  • Opportunistic Pathogens: High organic load allows bacteria that are normally harmless to multiply and cause disease in stressed fish.
  • Sporadic: Occurs infrequently in a few members of a population.
  • Epizootic: A large-scale outbreak in a limited geographical area.
  • Panzootic: An outbreak that occurs over a very large area.
  • Enzootic: Persists at a low level or re-occurs in a specific area.

V. Short Notes

1. Significance of Water Quality in Fish Health

Good water quality is fundamental to fish health management. The aquatic environment is dynamic, and fish, being poikilothermic, are directly affected by parameters like temperature, pH, and dissolved oxygen. Poor water quality acts as a major stressor, weakening the fish's immune system and making it susceptible to opportunistic pathogens. Key management practices include pre-treating inlet water, maintaining optimal dissolved oxygen through aeration, controlling organic load from uneaten feed and waste, and using biofilters in recirculating systems to remove toxic metabolites like ammonia.

2. The Role of Stress in Disease Development

Stress is a critical factor that disrupts the balance between the fish (host) and potential pathogens. When a fish is stressed by factors like high stocking density, poor water quality, or handling, its body initiates a physiological response via the Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Interrenal (HPI) axis. This leads to the release of stress hormones called corticosteroids. While these hormones help the fish cope in the short term, they have a significant downside: they suppress the immune system. This immunosuppression lowers the fish's natural disease resistance, opening the door for opportunistic pathogens to cause an infection.

VI. Long Answer Question

1. Explain the interaction between Host, Pathogen, and Environment in the development of fish diseases, as described by Snieszko. How do modern aquaculture practices influence this balance?

The development of disease in fish, according to S.F. Snieszko's model, results from a breakdown in the balance between three components: the Host, the Pathogen, and the Environment.

  • The Host: Refers to the fish's health, immune status, and stress level. A strong host can resist most infections.
  • The Pathogen: The disease-causing agent. Its ability to cause disease (virulence) and its concentration are key.
  • The Environment: The water conditions. Poor quality (e.g., low oxygen, high ammonia) stresses the host and can favor the pathogen.

Influence of Modern Aquaculture Practices: Modern intensive aquaculture disrupts this balance significantly. High stocking densities increase host stress and allow pathogens to spread easily. This also leads to water quality deterioration from accumulated waste, further stressing the fish. The high organic load creates an ideal breeding ground for opportunistic pathogens, increasing their concentration in the environment. In essence, intensive practices weaken the host while strengthening the pathogen and degrading the environment, making disease outbreaks far more likely.
