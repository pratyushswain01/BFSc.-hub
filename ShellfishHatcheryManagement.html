🧐 True or False

1. The Nauplius stage of shrimp larvae actively feeds on microalgae.▶

Answer: False. The nauplius is a non-feeding stage as it utilizes the reserved nutrients of the egg.

2. The Japanese system of shrimp production involves rearing larvae and culturing live feed in the same tank.▶

Answer: True. This system, also called "green water culture," involves spawning, hatching, and larval rearing in the same tank where microalgae (live feed) is also cultured.

3. The Y-organ secretes a Gonad Inhibiting Hormone (GIH).▶

Answer: False. The Y-organ secretes the moulting hormone ecdysone. GIH is secreted by the X-organ located in the eyestalk.

4. In crabs, mating (copulation) occurs between a hard-shelled male and a freshly moulted, soft-bodied female.▶

Answer: True. The courtship is initiated by a "premating embrace" between hard-shelled individuals, which lasts for 2-3 days, and copulation takes place after the female moults.

5. Lobster larvae have a very short larval life, lasting only a few days.▶

Answer: False. Lobsters have a lengthy larval life. The Phyllosoma stage alone can extend up to 250 days, during which the larvae undergo about a dozen moults.

6. Eyestalk ablation is performed on male crustaceans to induce spermatogenesis.▶

Answer: False. Eyestalk ablation is performed on females to remove the source of Gonad-Inhibiting Hormone (GIH) and induce ovarian maturation.

7. *Spirulina* has a soft cell wall and is highly digestible, making it a good feed for larvae.▶

Answer: True.

8. A "berried" female crab is one that has just finished moulting.▶

Answer: False. A "berried" female is one carrying fertilized eggs attached to her abdomen.

9. Bilateral eyestalk ablation (removing both eyestalks) is the preferred method as it has high survival rates and leads to successful spawning.▶

Answer: False. Bilateral ablation often leads to high mortality and reabsorption of ova; unilateral ablation is the preferred method.

10. In penaeid shrimps, the petasma is a modified reproductive structure found in females.▶

Answer: False. The petasma is found in males and is used for spermatophore transfer.

11. Thermal stimulation (raising the water temperature) can be used to induce spawning in sea cucumbers.▶

Answer: True.

12. *Artemia* cysts can be fed directly to larvae without hatching.▶

Answer: False. The hard outer shell (epi coat) of *Artemia* cysts is indigestible. The cysts must be hatched to release nauplii or decapsulated to create "Shell Free Artemia".

13. Cannibalism is a serious problem during the megalopa to crab I stage, especially at high stocking densities.▶

Answer: True.

14. The Galveston system of shrimp seed production was developed in Japan by Hudinaga.▶

Answer: False. The Galveston system was developed in the Galveston laboratory, USA. The Japanese system was developed by Hudinaga.

15. The freshwater prawn *Macrobrachium rosenbergii* has a direct life cycle, hatching as a miniature adult.▶

Answer: False. It has a complex life cycle with 11 distinct zoeal larval stages.

📝 Short Notes

1. The Galveston System▶

The Galveston system, also known as the "clear water rearing system," was developed at the Galveston laboratory, USA. Its key principle is the separation of processes: rearing of broodstock, spawning, hatching, larval rearing, and live feed culture are all conducted in separate tanks. Because live feeds are not cultured within the larval rearing tanks (LRTs), the water remains clear. This controlled environment leads to higher survival rates and more economical seed production compared to the Japanese system, making it the widely followed method in most countries.

2. Sexual Dimorphism in Penaeid Shrimps▶

Male and female penaeid shrimps are distinguished by several key external characters:

  • Male: Possesses a petasma on the 1st pleopod (swimming leg), which is a modified structure for transferring spermatophores. They also have an appendix masculina on the 2nd pleopod. The gonopore is located at the base of the 5th walking leg.
  • Female: Lacks a petasma and appendix masculina. They have a thelycum, a sperm receptacle located on the thoracic sternum between the 4th and 5th pairs of walking legs. The gonopore is at the base of the 3rd walking leg. Females are also generally larger than males of the same age group.
3. Eyestalk Ablation▶

Eyestalk ablation is the removal of one or both eyestalks in a crustacean. Its physiological purpose is to remove the X-organ-sinus-gland complex, which produces Gonad Inhibiting Hormone (GIH). By removing the source of this inhibitory hormone, the animal's own Gonad Stimulating Hormone (GSH) can act freely, inducing rapid ovarian maturation and spawning. Unilateral ablation (removing one eyestalk) is the standard practice as it provides sufficient hormonal stimulus without causing excessive mortality. Bilateral ablation (removing both) often leads to physiological stress, high mortality, and reabsorption of eggs.

4. Phyllosoma Larva▶

The Phyllosoma is the characteristic larval stage of spiny lobsters. It is transparent, flat, and leaf-shaped (phyllo = leaf, soma = body), a morphology adapted for a long planktonic life. They have very long, jointed appendages used for swimming and are dispersed far and wide by ocean currents. This stage is remarkably long, lasting from 90 to 250 days (or even up to 2 years in some species), during which the larva undergoes 7-13 moults before metamorphosing into the next stage, the Puerulus.

5. Spat Collection▶

In bivalves like oysters and mussels, "spat" refers to the juvenile stage when the larvae cease their planktonic life and settle onto a hard substrate. Spat collection is the practice of providing artificial substrates in natural waters during the peak spawning season to collect these settling juveniles for aquaculture. Common materials used as spat collectors include:
1. Lime-coated tiles: Semi-cylindrical roofing tiles coated with lime provide an attractive surface for oyster spat to attach.
2. Shell strings (Rens): Strings of old oyster or scallop shells are hung from rafts or racks, providing a natural and effective collection surface.
3. Rope collectors: Coir or synthetic ropes, often with untwisted filaments, offer a large surface area for mussel spat to attach.

6. Artemia as a Live Feed▶

*Artemia* (brine shrimp) is an indispensable live feed in shellfish hatcheries because its freshly hatched nauplii are the perfect size for the mouthparts of larval shrimp, prawns, and crabs. They are rich in protein and lipids, providing essential nutrients for growth and survival. A key advantage is that they are available as dormant cysts that can be stored for long periods and hatched on demand.
"Shell Free Artemia" are cysts that have been chemically treated (decapsulated) to remove the hard, indigestible outer shell. Their advantages include: 100% usability (no unhatched cysts), no need for a 24-hour hatching process, and higher energy content since the nauplius hasn't consumed its yolk sac reserves.

7. Water Quality in Hatcheries▶

Five critical water quality parameters are:
1. Dissolved Oxygen (DO): Essential for respiration. Low levels (<3-4 ppm) cause stress and mortality.
2. Temperature: Affects metabolic rate, growth, and moulting. Must be kept stable and within the optimal range for the species.
3. pH: Should be stable, typically between 7.5 and 8.5. Fluctuations affect physiological processes.
4. Salinity: Crucial for osmoregulation. Must match the specific requirements of the species and larval stage.
5. Ammonia (NH3): A toxic waste product. Levels must be kept extremely low (<0.01 ppm) as it can be lethal to larvae.

8. Biofiltration▶

Biofiltration is a biological water treatment process that uses beneficial bacteria to remove toxic nitrogenous wastes. In a hatchery, uneaten feed and animal excretion produce highly toxic ammonia (NH3). A biofilter provides a large surface area (using bio-balls, beads, etc.) for colonies of nitrifying bacteria to grow. The process occurs in two steps:
1. Bacteria of the genus *Nitrosomonas* consume ammonia and convert it into less toxic nitrite (NO2-).
2. Bacteria of the genus *Nitrobacter* then consume the nitrite and convert it into harmless nitrate (NO3-). This process, called nitrification, is essential for maintaining water quality in recirculating hatchery systems.

9. Spirulina▶

*Spirulina* is a blue-green alga (cyanobacterium) highly valued in aquaculture for its exceptional nutritional profile. It is a single-cell protein source containing 55-70% protein with an excellent amino acid profile. It is also rich in vitamins (especially B-12), minerals, and natural pigments like carotene and xanthophyll. A key advantage is its soft cell wall made of complex sugars, which makes it highly digestible for larval animals. In hatcheries, it is used as a supplementary feed to improve growth, survival, and pigmentation in shrimp and prawn larvae.

10. Open vs. Closed Thelycum▶

This classification is based on the morphology of the female shrimp's genital organ (thelycum) and dictates their reproductive strategy.
Open Thelycum Species (*Litopenaeus* subgenus, e.g., *P. vannamei*): The thelycum is a simple, external structure. The female must be sexually mature with a hardened exoskeleton to mate. The male deposits a spermatophore which is held externally for only a few hours before spawning. Their reproductive sequence is: molt -> mature -> mate -> spawn.
Closed Thelycum Species (*Penaeus* subgenus, e.g., *P. monodon*): The thelycum is a more complex internal structure with lateral plates that enclose a seminal receptacle. Mating can only occur immediately after the female molts, while her exoskeleton is still soft. The spermatophore is stored internally and protected as the shell hardens, and can be retained for one or several spawnings. Their sequence is: molt -> mate -> mature -> spawn.

📜 Long Answer Questions

1. Compare and contrast the Japanese (Green Water) system and the Galveston (Clear Water) system for shrimp seed production.▶

Japanese System (Green Water Culture):

  • Methodology: This was the first system developed, pioneered by Hudinaga. It involves conducting spawning, hatching, and larval rearing all within the same tank or pond. Live feed, primarily microalgae, is cultured simultaneously in the rearing tank, which gives the water a green color.
  • Advantages: Simpler in concept as it mimics a more natural, community-based environment.
  • Disadvantages: Aquatic conditions are uncontrolled, leading to fluctuations in water quality and algal density. This results in lower and less predictable larval survival rates.

Galveston System (Clear Water Culture):

  • Methodology: Developed in the USA, this system separates each stage of the production cycle. Broodstock rearing, spawning, hatching, larval rearing, and live feed culture are all performed in dedicated, separate tanks. Larvae are fed with live feeds (like Artemia and cultured algae) that are produced elsewhere and added to the larval rearing tank (LRT).
  • Advantages: Allows for precise control over water quality, feeding rates, and hygiene at each stage. This leads to significantly higher and more consistent larval survival rates and better overall economic efficiency.
  • Disadvantages: Requires more infrastructure (tanks, filters) and technical management compared to the Japanese system.

Conclusion: The Galveston system is widely preferred and followed globally because its controlled approach overcomes the unpredictability of the green water system, resulting in more reliable and commercially viable seed production.

2. Describe the complete life cycle of a mud crab (*Scylla serrata*), from a fertilized egg to the first crab instar.▶

The life cycle of the mud crab involves a planktonic larval phase before it settles as a juvenile crab. The development from a hatched egg takes approximately 23-31 days.
1. Egg to Zoea: After an incubation period of about 2 weeks, the fertilized eggs carried by the female ("berried" female) hatch into the first larval stage, the Zoea.
2. Zoeal Stages (5 stages): The zoea larva is planktonic and undergoes five successive moults. Each stage lasts 3-4 days.
    - Zoea I: Eyes are sessile (not on stalks). 5 abdominal segments. Telson has 3+3 spines.
    - Zoea II: Eyes become stalked. Telson now has 4+4 spines.
    - Zoea III: The abdomen develops a 6th segment.
    - Zoea IV: Pleopod buds (rudimentary swimming legs) appear on the abdominal segments.
    - Zoea V: Setae (bristles) appear on the pleopods. Telson has 5+5 spines.
3. Megalopa Stage: After the 5th zoeal stage, the larva metamorphoses into the Megalopa. This stage lasts 8-11 days and has a more crab-like appearance. The carapace is longer than it is wide, and it has well-developed chelipeds (claws) and four pairs of walking legs. It uses its five pairs of pleopods for swimming.
4. First Crab Instar: The Megalopa moults into the First Crab Instar, which is a miniature version of the adult crab. It has a carapace with nine anterolateral spines on each side and its last pair of legs has a paddle-shaped dactylus. At this point, it settles to the bottom and begins its benthic life.

3. Explain the neuroendocrine control of reproduction in crustaceans.▶

Reproduction in crustaceans is primarily controlled by a complex interplay of hormones secreted by several glands, operating through a system of stimulation and inhibition:

  • X-Organ-Sinus-Gland Complex: Located in the eyestalks, this is the primary inhibitory center. It secretes several neurohormones, most importantly the Gonad Inhibiting Hormone (GIH) and Moult Inhibiting Hormone (MIH). GIH (also called OIH or VIH) actively suppresses ovarian development and vitellogenesis (yolk formation).
  • Thoracic Ganglia: This gland secretes Gonad Stimulating Hormone (GSH), which promotes the development of gonads, gametogenesis, and prepares the animal for breeding. The secretion of GSH is believed to be triggered by a releasing factor from the supra-oesophageal ganglia.
  • Y-Organ: Located in the antennary/maxillary segment, this gland secretes the moulting hormone ecdysone. Its activity is inhibited by MIH from the X-organ. Since moulting and reproduction are often linked, this creates an indirect reproductive control pathway.
  • Mandibular Organ: This organ synthesizes methyl farnesoate (MF), a substance that has a stimulatory effect on the Y-organ, encouraging it to secrete ecdysone, thereby promoting moulting and indirectly influencing the reproductive cycle.
  • Androgenic Gland (Males only): This gland secretes androgenic hormone (AH), which is crucial for controlling all primary and secondary male sexual characteristics, including spermatogenesis. Its presence determines the development of testes; its absence allows for the development of an ovary.

The overall process is a balance: GSH promotes maturation, while GIH from the eyestalks holds it in check. This is why eyestalk ablation is an effective technique in hatcheries to induce maturation by removing the source of the inhibitory hormone.

4. Detail the process and importance of eyestalk ablation in shrimp hatcheries.▶

Importance: Many commercially important shrimp species, like *P. monodon*, do not readily mature and spawn in captivity. This is because the X-organ-sinus-gland complex in their eyestalks produces Gonad Inhibiting Hormone (GIH), which suppresses ovarian development. Eyestalk ablation is a crucial hatchery technique used to remove this source of inhibition, thereby allowing the shrimp's natural Gonad Stimulating Hormone (GSH) to promote rapid maturation of the ovaries, leading to spawning. It is an essential prerequisite for captive breeding programs.

Process and Methods: The procedure is performed only on inter-molt females. To minimize stress and mortality, the shrimp is often chilled before the procedure. The standard practice is unilateral ablation (removing only one eyestalk). Common methods include:
1. Electrocauterization: The eyestalk is cut near the base with scissors, and the wound is immediately sealed with a heated instrument (like a pencil-type soldering iron) to prevent bleeding (hemolymph loss) and infection. This is a very effective method.
2. Enucleation (Pinching): The eyestalk is grasped firmly behind the eyeball and squeezed hard, forcing the contents of the eye out. This method is simple and promotes rapid clotting within the empty eyestalk.
3. Ligation: A string is tied tightly around the base of the eyestalk, cutting off circulation and causing it to fall off within a few days. This method avoids an open wound but is slower and less predictable.

Effects and Drawbacks: After successful ablation, ovarian development can begin within 3 days, with a first spawn possible within a week. However, there are drawbacks. The resulting eggs and larvae are often considered inferior in quality and viability compared to those from wild-matured females. The hormonal imbalance can lead to improper nutrient storage, lower embryonic development, and reduced juvenile survival.

5. Outline a complete seed production protocol for the tiger shrimp, *Penaeus monodon*.▶

A seed production protocol for *P. monodon* follows the Galveston (clear water) system:
1. Broodstock Management: Healthy, wild-caught female brooders (70-150g, average 120g) are sourced. They are acclimated and disinfected (e.g., 100 ppm formalin dip) before being placed in maturation tanks. Unilateral eyestalk ablation is performed to induce maturation.
2. Spawning: Ablated females are stocked with males (ratio 1:1 or 1.5:1) in maturation tanks. After mating, impregnated females are transferred to individual spawning tanks. A single female can produce 8-10 lakhs (0.8-1 million) eggs. Hatching occurs in the early morning, with a hatchability rate of 95-100%.
3. Larval Rearing (in LRTs):
    - Nauplius (N1-N6): Lasts 36-48 hours. This is a non-feeding stage.
    - Protozoea (Z1-Z3): Lasts 3-4 days. This is the first feeding stage.
    - Mysis (M1-M3): Lasts 3-4 days.
    - Post-Larvae (PL): The Mysis III metamorphoses into PL1. The total larval rearing duration is about 13-15 days.
4. Feeding Regime:
    - Zoea Stage (Day 1-5): Fed exclusively on microalgae like *Chaetoceros* or *Skeletonema* at a density of 100,000 cells/ml.
    - Mysis Stage (Day 6-10): Continue with microalgae and introduce freshly hatched *Artemia* nauplii, starting at 0.25 nauplii/ml and increasing to 0.5 nauplii/ml.
    - Post-Larval Stage (Day 11+): The microalgae density is reduced (e.g., to 60,000 cells/ml), and the *Artemia* density is increased to 1 nauplius/ml. Artificial feeds (microencapsulated diets or egg custard) are also introduced.
5. Water Management: Water exchange starts at 30% daily during the Zoea stage and increases to 70-100% during the Mysis and PL stages to maintain optimal water quality.
6. Harvest: With an average survival rate of 20%, a single 100g breeder can yield 1.0 to 2.5 lakhs (100,000 to 250,000) PLs per cycle.

6. Discuss the challenges and management strategies for health and sanitation in a shellfish hatchery.▶

Challenges: Shellfish hatcheries are high-density environments where organic inputs (feed) create ideal conditions for pathogenic microbes. Larval stages are highly vulnerable to diseases, which can cause mass mortality and complete crop failure. Common challenges include:
    - Bacterial Diseases: Luminous bacterial disease (*Vibrio harveyi*), Vibriosis, and Bacterial Necrosis can affect all larval stages.
    - Fungal Diseases: Caused by fungi like *Lagenidium*, which are particularly dangerous to Mysis stages.
    - Viral Diseases: Baculovirus and Parvovirus (IHHNV) can cause high mortality, especially in post-larval stages.
    - Larval Fouling: Epibionts like *Zoothamnium* and *Vorticella* can attach to gills and appendages, causing hypoxia and death.

Management Strategies (Disinfection & Prevention): The best strategy is prevention through strict sanitation and disinfection to break the life cycle of pathogens between production cycles. This involves a combination of physical and chemical methods:
    - Physical Methods:
        • Cleaning & Dehydration: Thoroughly scrubbing and drying all tanks, pipes, and equipment kills fragile pathogens and removes the organic substrate they grow on.
        • Filtration: Mechanical and biological filtration of incoming and recirculating water removes suspended solids and associated microbes.
        • UV Radiation: Treating water with UV light (2500-2650 Å) is highly effective at destroying viruses, bacteria, and fungi without leaving chemical residues.
    - Chemical Methods:
        • Chlorination: Treating water with calcium or sodium hypochlorite is a common and effective method, but residual chlorine must be completely removed (e.g., by aeration or charcoal filtration) before use.
        • Formalin: Used as a dip for brooders and to disinfect equipment and water.
        • Ozonation: Highly effective but requires expensive equipment. Residual ozone must also be removed.
        • Quaternary Ammonium Compounds (QACs) & Iodophores: Used for disinfecting surfaces and equipment.

7. Describe the process of hatchery-based seed production for holothurians (sea cucumbers).▶

Hatchery production of holothurians (*Holothuria scabra*) is crucial for replenishing over-exploited wild stocks. The process involves several key steps:
1. Broodstock Management: Large, healthy adults (>150mm) are collected by divers from the wild. In the hatchery, they are maintained in tanks with a 15cm mud/sand substrate to allow for natural burying behavior. Water is changed daily.
2. Induced Spawning: Spawning is induced using thermal stimulation. During the natural breeding season (March-May), the water temperature in the spawning tank is raised by 3-5°C. This prompts the males to spawn first, releasing sperm while adopting a "cobra-like" posture. The presence of sperm in the water then stimulates the females to release their oocytes.
3. Fertilization: Fertilization is external. After spawning, the adults are removed, and the eggs are washed several times with fresh seawater to remove excess sperm, which can cause deformities.
4. Larval Rearing: The fertilized eggs develop through several distinct planktonic larval stages.
    - Auricularia: Hatches after ~24-30 hours. This is the first feeding stage. The larvae are fed microalgae like *Isochrysis galbana* and *Chaetoceros* at a density of 20,000-30,000 cells/ml. This stage lasts about 10 days.
    - Doliolaria: The late auricularia transforms into a non-feeding, barrel-shaped doliolaria larva with ciliary bands for swimming. This is a transitional stage lasting a few days.
    - Pentactula: The doliolaria develops into a pentactula larva, which has five tentacles at its anterior end and a tube foot for locomotion. This stage begins to settle.
5. Settlement and Juvenile Rearing: The pentactula larvae settle on the bottom of the tank and metamorphose into juvenile sea cucumbers. They are very secretive and require suitable substrates to hide. Growth is slow, and they are reared in the hatchery until they are large enough for sea ranching.
