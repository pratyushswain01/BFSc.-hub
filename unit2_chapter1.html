Review the key concepts from Chapter 1 on Pathophysiology. Click on any topic to expand and see more details.

  • Pathology: The study of disease and their causative agents.
  • Obligate Pathogen: An organism that will cause disease simply by being present (e.g., *Aeromonas salmonicida*).
  • Opportunistic Pathogen: An organism that only causes disease when the host's resistance is low (e.g., *Vibrio sp.* in shrimps, *Aeromonas hydrophila*).
  • Stress: Defined by Selye (1950) as the sum of all physiological responses by which an animal tries to maintain normal metabolism in the face of a physical or chemical force. Temperature is a major stressor for fish.

GAS describes the three phases of an animal's response to stress:

  • 1. Alarm Reaction: The initial physical response to a stressor, like escaping or physiological changes (e.g., fish coming to the surface for oxygen).
  • 2. Stage of Resistance: The period where the animal adapts to achieve homeostasis (internal balance) under the new, stressful conditions.
  • 3. Stage of Exhaustion: If the animal fails to achieve homeostasis, it becomes exhausted, which can lead to disease.
  • Hormonal Control: The GAS is mediated by the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Interrenal (HPI) axis, which releases hormones like Cortisol and ACTH.

Inflammation is the series of changes in tissue following an injury. The five cardinal signs in higher vertebrates are:

  • Rubor (Redness): Due to dilation of capillaries.
  • Tumor (Swelling): Due to fluid (exudate) leaking from capillaries.
  • Calor (Heat): Due to increased physical activity in the area.
  • Dolor (Pain): Due to stretching of nerve endings.
  • Et functio laesa (Loss of function): Due to a combination of pain, swelling, and tissue destruction.
  • Cellular Response: White blood cells like Neutrophils and Monocytes migrate from the blood to the affected tissue.

Necrosis is the death of cells, a non-reversible outcome of prolonged inflammation.

  • Types of Necrosis:
    • Liquefactive Necrosis: Enzymatic digestion of cells (e.g., in furunculosis).
    • Coagulative Necrosis: Results from loss of blood supply (ischaemia).
    • Fat Necrosis: Rare; caused by lipase enzymes from damaged pancreatic tissue.
  • Stages of Necrosis (Nuclear Changes):
    • Pyknosis: Shrunken, dark nucleus.
    • Karyorhexis: Rupture and fragmentation of the nucleus.
    • Karyolysis: Complete dissolution of the nucleus.
  • Granuloma: A mass of inflamed tissue, often associated with chronic inflammation, formed to wall off a foreign body or pathogen like *Mycobacterium*.

V. Short Notes

1. Differentiate between Obligate and Opportunistic Pathogens.

An **obligate pathogen** is an organism that will cause disease simply by its presence in the culture system, as it is inherently virulent (e.g., *Aeromonas salmonicida*). In contrast, an **opportunistic pathogen** is an organism that is normally harmless or a commensal but can cause disease when the host's resistance is lowered due to factors like stress or poor water quality (e.g., *Aeromonas hydrophila*).

2. Explain the three stages of Necrosis based on nuclear changes.

Necrosis, or cell death, can be identified by observing changes in the cell's nucleus. The three stages are:
1. Pyknosis: The initial stage where the nucleus shrinks and becomes very dark (haematoxylinophilic).
2. Karyorhexis: The second stage where the nuclear membrane ruptures and the chromatin fragments.
3. Karyolysis: The final stage where the nucleus completely dissolves as its nucleic acids are hydrolyzed, losing their basophilic staining properties.

VI. Long Answer Question

1. Describe the General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) in fish. Explain the three phases and the hormonal axis that regulates this response.

The General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) is the sum of all physiological responses a fish undergoes to maintain or re-establish normal metabolism when faced with a stressor. This response is non-specific, meaning various stressors like infection, fright, or poor water quality can trigger a similar set of reactions. The GAS occurs in three distinct phases:

  • Alarm Reaction: This is the immediate, initial response to the stressor. It involves physical reactions like attempting to escape or physiological changes such as moving to the water surface if dissolved oxygen is low. It's the body's first alert to a change in conditions.
  • Stage of Resistance: If the stressor persists, the fish enters this stage. Here, the body actively adapts to achieve homeostasis (a stable internal state) under the new, challenging circumstances. Hormonal and metabolic changes occur to help the fish cope with the ongoing stress.
  • Stage of Exhaustion: If the fish is unable to adapt and achieve homeostasis during the resistance stage, it enters exhaustion. In this final phase, the fish's resources are depleted, and its physiological systems begin to fail. This stage often leads to a compromised immune system and the onset of disease.

Hormonal Regulation: The entire GAS process is regulated by the **Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Interrenal (HPI) axis**. The hypothalamus senses the stressor and activates the pituitary gland. The pituitary, in turn, secretes tropic factors that activate the interrenal tissue (the equivalent of the adrenal gland in mammals) to release stress hormones, primarily **Cortisol** and **ACTH**. These hormones drive the physiological changes seen throughout the GAS stages.
