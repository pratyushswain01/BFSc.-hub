Unit 2, Chapter 2 Study Hub

Review the key pathologies affecting different organ systems in fish. Click on any system to expand and see more details.

  • Structure: Comprises Cuticle, Epidermis, Basement membrane, Dermis, Hypodermis, and Subcutaneous muscle. The epidermis is living and avascular.
  • Spongiosis (Intercellular Oedema): The earliest inflammatory sign; separation of malpighian cells by tissue fluid.
  • Hyperplasia: Abnormal increase in the number of cells, a common response in fish epidermis, especially at low temperatures.
  • Wound Ulcers: Caused by injury or infection, leading to a break in the skin. Healing is temperature-dependent and involves migration of malpighian cells.

Gills are delicate and a primary site for infection and damage from irritants. Three main pathological changes are recognized:

  • 1. Lamellar Oedema: Reversible fluid accumulation in the lamella, often due to chemical pollutants.
  • 2. Lamellar Hyperplasia: Increase in the number of malpighian cells, thickening the lamella and reducing gas exchange efficiency.
  • 3. Lamellar Fusion: Irreversible fusion of secondary lamellae, severely reducing the respiratory surface area.
  • Haemopoietic Tissue: Located in the spleen and kidney, responsible for producing blood cells.
  • Anaemia: Reduction in red blood cells. Three types:
    • Haemorrhagic: Due to excessive blood loss (e.g., from VHSV).
    • Haemolytic: Due to increased destruction of red blood cells (e.g., by *Vibrio* toxins).
    • Hypoplastic: Due to failure of haemopoietic tissue to produce cells (e.g., nutritional deficiency).
  • Heart Pathologies: Include Oedema (cardiac failure), Myocardial Necrosis (muscle degeneration), and nutritional myopathies (Vitamin E deficiency).
  • Oral Cavity: Can suffer from traumatic lesions, ulcers, and conditions like Enteric Red Mouth disease (*Yersinia ruckeri*).
  • Intestine: Can be damaged by protozoans (coccidiosis), nematodes, or toxins, leading to sloughing of the gut mucosa.
  • Liver Pathologies:
    • Hepatic Necrosis: Death of liver cells.
    • Lipid Infiltration (Lipoid Liver): Excess fat accumulation, often from rancid feed.
    • Cirrhosis: Increase in fibrous tissue due to chronic damage.
    • Hepatoma (Carcinoma): Liver cancer, often caused by Aflatoxin from *Aspergillus flavus* mold.
  • Pancreas Pathologies:
    • Acinar Necrosis: Death of enzyme-producing cells, a key sign of Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis Virus (IPNV).
    • Pancreatic Atrophy: Shrinkage of pancreatic cells.

V. Short Notes

1. Describe the three main pathological changes observed in fish gills.

The three main pathological changes in fish gills are:
1. Lamellar Oedema: This is a reversible condition where fluid accumulates in the interstitial spaces of the lamellae, causing them to swell. It is often a response to chemical pollutants and can lead to respiratory distress.
2. Lamellar Hyperplasia: This is an increase in the number of epithelial (malpighian) cells on the lamellae. This thickens the tissue, which reduces the efficiency of gas exchange and can interfere with ion regulation.
3. Lamellar Fusion: This is an irreversible, severe outcome of massive hyperplasia where adjacent secondary lamellae fuse together into a solid mass. This drastically reduces the available surface area for respiration and can be fatal, especially as oxygen demand increases.

2. Explain the three types of anaemia found in fish.

Anaemia is a reduction in the total mass of red blood cells, leading to deficient oxygen transport. The three types are:
1. Haemorrhagic Anaemia: Caused by excessive blood loss that outpaces the body's ability to replace it. This can result from physical injury, certain viral diseases (like VHSV), or blood-sucking parasites.
2. Haemolytic Anaemia: Caused by an increased rate of red blood cell destruction (haemolysis). This is often due to toxins (haemolysins) produced by bacteria like *Vibrio anguillarum* or certain blood protozoa.
3. Hypoplastic Anaemia: Caused by the failure of the haemopoietic tissues (in the kidney and spleen) to produce an adequate number of red blood cells. This can be due to nutritional deficiencies, radiation, or diseases affecting these organs.

VI. Long Answer Question

1. Compare and contrast the pathological responses of the fish integumentary system (skin) and respiratory system (gills) to external stressors and pathogens.

The skin and gills are the two primary barriers between a fish and its environment, and both exhibit distinct pathological responses to stressors and pathogens.

Integumentary System (Skin): The skin's response is primarily protective and reparative. Its earliest inflammatory response is **Spongiosis** (oedema), where fluid separates the epidermal cells. A more common and generalized response is **Hyperplasia**, an increase in cell number to thicken the epidermis as a protective barrier. If the barrier is breached by trauma or a lesion, an **ulcer** forms. The skin's healing process is highly effective but temperature-dependent, relying on the migration of **malpighian cells** to cover the wound. Because the epidermis is avascular, its direct responses are limited, and major inflammatory events occur deeper in the vascularized dermis and hypodermis.

Respiratory System (Gills): The gills, being extremely delicate and directly involved in vital functions (respiration, osmoregulation), show more severe and functionally debilitating responses. Like the skin, gills can exhibit **oedema** (Lamellar Oedema) and **hyperplasia** (Lamellar Hyperplasia). However, gill hyperplasia is more immediately dangerous as it thickens the lamellar epithelium, directly impeding gas exchange. The most severe gill response is **Lamellar Fusion**, an irreversible consequence of massive hyperplasia where the respiratory surfaces are permanently lost. Unlike the skin, where healing aims to restore a simple barrier, pathological changes in the gills directly compromise a critical physiological function, often leading to severe respiratory distress and mortality.

Comparison: Both systems use hyperplasia as a key defensive response. However, the consequences are vastly different. Skin hyperplasia is primarily a thickening defense, while gill hyperplasia is a life-threatening impediment to breathing. Skin damage (ulcers) can be effectively repaired, whereas severe gill damage (fusion) is irreversible. Therefore, while both systems act as barriers, the pathological responses of the gills are more functionally critical and have more immediate and severe consequences for the fish's survival.
